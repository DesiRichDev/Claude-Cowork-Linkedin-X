# Structure Rules

How posts are built. These apply to all platforms unless the platform rules override.

---

## Hook Rule

First two lines: max 8 words each.
This forces the mobile "see more" click on LinkedIn.
Second line must "recook" the hook — add a twist, deepen the intrigue, or create contrast.

Good:
```
Her company was worth $3M yesterday.
Her personal brand is worth $30M today.
```

Bad:
```
Here are five things I learned about building a personal brand on LinkedIn in 2026.
You might be surprised by what actually works.
```

---

## Paragraph Structure

Never single lines stacked with no grouping.
Never a wall of text with no breaks.

Use this pattern:

```
Hook line 1 — max 8 words
Hook line 2 — max 8 words

2-3 sentences grouped. Context, why this matters.

Core insight. 1-2 sentences.
What changes because of this.

Supporting detail or example.
2-3 sentences max per group.

Closing question or sharp one-liner.
```

---

## Post Frameworks

STORY — something that happened, lesson at the end
- Hook with unexpected outcome
- Set the scene
- The challenge or conflict
- What shifted
- The result
- The lesson for readers
- Closing question

LESSON — breakdown of a concept or pattern, practical only
- Hook with data or counterintuitive claim
- Context and why it matters
- Numbered breakdown with specifics inside each point
- Sharp closer

EDU-TELLING — actionable insight wrapped in a real example
- Hook with specific outcome
- Real example that shows the insight in action
- Extract the pattern
- What readers can apply
- Closing question

YOU — direct to reader, challenges their current thinking
- Permission opener or bold direct statement
- Call out the contradiction or absurdity
- Reframe with specifics
- Consequence or opportunity
- Rhetorical question or mic drop closer

---

## Character Limits (hard)

Always count before delivering. These are hard ceilings.

- LinkedIn: 2100 chars
- X: 280 chars (including spaces and line breaks)
- Instagram: 2200 chars
- Facebook: 1500 chars

---

## Closing Types That Work

- A question the reader has to answer about themselves
- A sharp one-liner reframe
- A bold claim or guarantee
- A simple two-word contrast
- A direct challenge
